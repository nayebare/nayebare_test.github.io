Research Papers

  • Peer Group Composition and Academic Outcomes: Exploring the Impact of Coethnic and High-Ability Peers in Uganda’s University Setting Market Paper

    This paper explores the effects of two types of peers in a group, coethnic and high ability, on academic outcomes at a large public university in Uganda, a setting with a high degree of ethnic heterogeneity. I link student-level proprietary data at this university, where living arrangements are randomly assigned conditional on gender and admission. The results show that the share of coethnic peers in a peer group does not affect academic outcomes on average. In contrast, the share of high-ability peers positively affects academic performance, irrespective of ethnicity. However, students experiencing a ‘culture shock’ (those that have lived and studied high school in their ethnic regions prior to college) benefit from having a higher share of coethnic peers. This preliminary evidence suggests psychological mechanisms, other than ethnic-based sorting into study groups, drive these results.
